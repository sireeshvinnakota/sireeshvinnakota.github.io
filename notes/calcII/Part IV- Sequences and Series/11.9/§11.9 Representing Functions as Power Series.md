Note that, from [[§11.2 Series#^df9d00|geometric series]], we have:$$
\frac{1}{1-x} =1 + x + x ^2 + \cdots =\sum_{n = 0}^\infty x^n \quad \text{ whenever } \quad \left | x \right | < 1$$just by interpreting the $x$ as $r$ in that equation. This will be the key observation in putting an arbitrary function $f(x)$ into the form of [[§11.8 Power Series]] 

> [!theorem] Theorem
> If the power series $\Sigma c_n(x-a)^n$ has radius of convergence $R>0$, then the function $f$ defined by
> $$f(x)=c_0+c_1(x-a)+c_2(x-a)^2+\cdots=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$$
> is differentiable (and therefore continuous) on the interval $(a-R, a+R)$ and
> - $f^{\prime}(x)=c_1+2 c_2(x-a)+3 c_3(x-a)^2+\cdots=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_n(x-a)^{n-1}$
> - $\int f(x) d x=C+c_0(x-a)+c_1 \frac{(x-a)^2}{2}+c_2 \frac{(x-a)^3}{3}+\cdots$
> $$=C+\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n \frac{(x-a)^{n+1}}{n+1}$$
> 
> The radii of convergence of the power series in Equations (i) and (ii) are both $R$.
