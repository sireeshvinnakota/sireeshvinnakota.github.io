Calculus II — §11.8 Power Series
← Back to Calc II graph  

[!definition] Definition (Power Series) A power series is a series of the form: $$ \sum_{n =0}^\infty c_n x^n = c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots $$ such that, for each value of $x$, we can test for convergence or divergence. More generally, the series: $$ \sum_{n =0}^\infty c_n (x-a)^n = c_0 + c_1 (x-a) + c_2 (x-a)^2 + \cdots $$ is a power series centered at $x = a$.
[!theorem] Theorem For a given power series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$, there are only three possibilities:
  • The series converges only when $x=a$.
  • The series converges for all $x$.
  • There is a positive number $R$ such that the series converges if $|x-a|R$.
In the instance of $(iii)$, we call $R$ the radius of convergence.
[!example] Example Determine the radius of convergence of the following power series centered at $a = 0$: $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty n! x^n $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> Whenever we see a factorial, our heads should immediately think: [[§11.6 Ratio and Root Tests#^ab1e80|ratio test:]] >> $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right | = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | \frac{(n+1)!x^n}{n! x^n} \right | = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | (n + 1)x \right | < \infty $$ >> where the last inequality holds if and only if $x = 0$.
[!example] Example Determine the radius of convergence and interval of convergence of the following power series. $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{n(x+2)^n}{3^{n+1}} $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> Note first that this series is centered at $a = -2$, so our interval of convergence will require an additional step. We’ll use the [[§11.6 Ratio and Root Tests#^ab1e80|ratio test]] >> $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | \frac{a_{n +1}}{a_n} \right | = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | \frac{n+1 (x + 2)^{n+1}}{3^{n+2}} \cdot \frac{3^{n+1}}{n(x+2)^n} \right | = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left | \frac{n+1}{3n}(x+2) \right | = \frac{\left | x+2 \right |}{3} $$ >> Using the conclusion of the Ratio test, we see that: >> $$ \sum_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{n(x+2)^n}{3^{n+1}} \begin{cases} \text{converges}, &\text{ if } \frac{\left | x+2 \right |}{3} < 1\\ \text{diverges}, &\text{ if } \frac{\left | x+2 \right |}{3} > 1\\ \end{cases} $$ >> So we conclude that our $R = 3$ and the interval of convergence is $(-5, 1)$. As an exercise, test the points $-5, 1$ to see that the series doesn’t converge at either, so we need not add either point to our interval of convergence.
