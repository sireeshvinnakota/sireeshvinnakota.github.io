Calculus II — §5.4 Indefinite Integrals
← Back to Calc II graph  

The big idea in this section is just to remember why we have to use $+C$ when we are taking antiderivatives: both $x^2 + 3$ and $x^2 + 5$ have the same derivative, even though they’re different functions. In general, derivatives tend to lose some information, and the $C$ accommodates for all possibilities of that lost constant. These functions with general constants

>[!fact] Theorem (eluR rewoP)
>$\int x^n dx = \frac{1}{n+1}x^{n+1}$

[!example] Example Complete the following table of antiderivatives $$ > \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|} > \hline > \text{Function} & \text{Antiderivative} & \text{Function} & \text{Antiderivative}\\ \hline > x^n & & \frac{1}{x} & \\ > C & & e^x & \\ > \cos(x) & & \sec^2(x) & \\ > \csc(x)\cot(x) & & \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & \\ > \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} & & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & \\ \hline > \end{array} > $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> $$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|}\hline\text{Function} & \text{Antiderivative} & \text{Function} & \text{Antiderivative}\\ \hline x^n & \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} +C & \frac{1}{x} & \ln|x| + C \\ C & Cx + D & e^x & e^x + C\\ \cos(x) & \sin(x) + C& \sec^2(x) & \tan(x) + C \\ \csc(x)\cot(x) & -\csc(x) + C & \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & \arcsin(x) + C \\ \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^2-1}} & \sec^{-1}(x) + C & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & \arccos (x) + C \\ \hline\end{array}$$
[!example] Problem Compute $f(x)$ when: $$ > f''(x) = e^x -2\sin (x), \quad f(0)=3 \quad \text{ and} \quad f(\pi/2) = 0 > $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> We integrate: >> $$\int e^x -2 \sin(x) dx = e^x + 2\cos(x) + C = f’(x)$$ >> ...and once more >> $$\int e^x + 2\cos(x) + C dx = e^x + 2\sin(x) + Cx + D = f(x)$$ >> Now, using the $f(0)= 3$, we test: >> $$f(0) = e^0 + 2\sin(0) + C\cdot 0 + D = 1 + D = 3$$ >> implying $D = 2$. Similarly, we use $f(\pi/2) = 0$ to find: >> $$f(\pi/2) = e^{\pi/2} + 2\sin(\pi/2) + C \cdot \pi/2 + 2 = 0$$ >> implying that >> $$C = -\frac{2}{\pi} \left ( e^{\pi/2} +4 \right )$$ >> This gives us the final answer: >> $$\boxed{f(x) = e^x + 2\sin(x) + -\left ( \frac{2}{\pi} (e^{\pi/2} +4) \right )x + 2}$$
[!example] Problem Compute the indefinite integral: $$ > \int \frac{\cos x}{\sin^2 x} dx > $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> We know that $\frac{\cos}{\sin} = \cot$, and what is left is a factor of $\frac1\sin = \csc$: >> $$\int \csc x \cot x dx = -\csc x + C$$
[!example] Problem Evaluate the following definite integral: $$ > \int_0^2 x \left ( \sqrt[3]{x} + \sqrt[4]{x} \right ) dx > $$
>> [!success]- Solution >> We compute the general antiderivative:$$\int x \left ( \sqrt[3]{x} + \sqrt[4]{x} \right ) dx = \int x^{4/3} + x^{5/4}dx$$ >> When we take the “reverse power rule” we have: >> $$\frac37 x^{7/3} + \frac49 x^{9/4} + C$$ and plug in:$$[\frac37 x^{7/3} + \frac49 x^{9/4} + C]_0^2 = 2^{7/3} + 2^{9/4}$$ >
